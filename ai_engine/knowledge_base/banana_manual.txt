AGROTRACK GUIDE: BANANA FARMING (KENYA)

==================================================
SECTION 1: VARIETY SELECTION
==================================================

COOKING BANANAS (PLANTAINS):
- Matoke varieties: Most common in Western Kenya
- Mbidde: Large bunches, good for cooking
- Mpologoma: Sweet cooking banana
- Musa Paradisiaca: Traditional plantain

DESSERT BANANAS (SWEET):
- Williams/Cavendish: Commercial variety, export quality
- Grand Nain: High yielding, disease susceptible
- FHIA varieties (FHIA 17, FHIA 23): Resistant to Black Sigatoka
- Apple Banana: Sweet, good for local market
- Dwarf Cavendish: Suitable for small-scale, 2-2.5m height

DUAL PURPOSE:
- Sukali Ndiizi: Sweet when ripe, can cook when green
- Bokoboko: Popular in Central and Nyanza

TISSUE CULTURE vs SUCKERS:
- Tissue culture: Disease-free, uniform, faster maturity, expensive
- Suckers: Cheaper, may carry diseases, variable quality
- Always use certified planting material

==================================================
SECTION 2: SOIL & SITE REQUIREMENTS
==================================================

SOIL REQUIREMENTS:
- Deep, well-drained loamy soils (1m+ depth)
- pH: 5.5-7.0 (slightly acidic to neutral)
- Rich in organic matter
- Avoid waterlogged or sandy soils

CLIMATE:
- Altitude: 1,000-2,000m above sea level
- Temperature: 20-30°C (optimum 27°C)
- Rainfall: 1,000-2,000mm well distributed
- Avoid areas with strong winds

BEST REGIONS IN KENYA:
- Western: Kakamega, Bungoma, Busia
- Nyanza: Kisii, Kisumu, Homa Bay
- Central: Muranga, Nyeri (lower zones)
- Coast: Kilifi, Kwale, Taita Taveta

==================================================
SECTION 3: LAND PREPARATION & PLANTING
==================================================

LAND PREPARATION:
- Clear land of weeds and debris
- Deep ploughing (30cm minimum)
- Dig planting holes: 60cm × 60cm × 60cm
- Fill holes with topsoil mixed with 20kg well-decomposed manure

SPACING:
- High-density: 2m × 2m (1,089 plants per acre)
- Medium-density: 2.5m × 2.5m (696 plants per acre)
- Low-density: 3m × 3m (484 plants per acre)
- Recommended: 2.5m × 2.5m for good management

PLANTING TIME:
- Beginning of rainy season (optimal)
- Long rains: March-April
- Short rains: October-November
- Can plant year-round with irrigation

PLANTING PROCEDURE:
- Use tissue culture plantlets or healthy sword suckers (1-1.5m tall)
- Remove all roots and trim to white tissue
- Treat with hot water (55°C for 10 minutes) to kill pests
- Plant at same depth as in nursery
- Firm soil around plant and water thoroughly
- Mulch with banana trash or other organic matter

==================================================
SECTION 4: FERTILIZER APPLICATION
==================================================

PLANTING FERTILIZER:
- 20-30kg well-decomposed manure per hole
- 200g TSP (Triple Super Phosphate) per hole
- Mix thoroughly with topsoil

YEAR 1 (ESTABLISHMENT):
- Month 1: 50g CAN per plant
- Month 3: 100g CAN per plant
- Month 6: 100g CAN + 50g NPK 17:17:17
- Month 9: 100g CAN per plant

YEAR 2 ONWARDS (MATURE PLANTATION):
- Every 3 months: 200g CAN per mat
- Every 6 months: 150g NPK 17:17:17 per mat
- Annual manure: 20kg per mat
- Apply in ring around plant, 30cm from pseudostem

MICRONUTRIENTS:
- Foliar spray with zinc and boron
- Important for bunch development
- Apply monthly during flowering

ORGANIC OPTIONS:
- 30-50kg well-decomposed manure per mat per year
- Compost from banana trash
- Coffee husks (in coffee-banana systems)
- Green manure from cover crops

==================================================
SECTION 5: IRRIGATION & WATER MANAGEMENT
==================================================

WATER REQUIREMENTS:
- Bananas need 25-30mm water per week
- Critical periods: Flowering and bunch development
- Susceptible to both drought and waterlogging

IRRIGATION METHODS:
- Drip irrigation: Most efficient
- Furrow irrigation: Common but water-intensive
- Basin irrigation: For small-scale
- Mulching: Conserves moisture significantly

IRRIGATION SCHEDULE:
- Dry season: Irrigate 2-3 times per week
- Young plants: More frequent light irrigation
- Mature plants: Deep watering less frequently

DRAINAGE:
- Dig trenches in waterlogged areas
- Raise beds in heavy soils
- Ensure good surface drainage

==================================================
SECTION 6: DESUCKERING & MAT MANAGEMENT
==================================================

DESUCKERING (CRITICAL PRACTICE):
- Keep only 3 stems per mat: 
  * Mother (bearing)
  * Follower (will fruit next)
  * Sword sucker (replacement)
- Remove all other suckers every 6-8 weeks
- Cut suckers at ground level using sharp panga
- Remove completely to prevent regrowth

WHY DESUCKERING IS IMPORTANT:
- Prevents overcrowding
- Better bunch size and quality
- Easier pest and disease management
- Optimal use of nutrients and water
- Allows air circulation

SUCKER SELECTION:
- Choose strong sword suckers (narrow leaves)
- Water suckers (broad leaves) produce smaller bunches
- Position: Select suckers away from parent plant

==================================================
SECTION 7: PEST & DISEASE MANAGEMENT
==================================================

MAJOR PESTS:
- Banana Weevil (Cosmopolites): Most serious pest, bores into corm
  Control: Use pheromone traps, clean planting material, remove trash
  Chemical: Apply Chlorpyrifos granules at base
- Nematodes: Cause toppling, reduced yield
  Control: Use tissue culture, crop rotation, soil fumigation
- Banana Aphid: Transmit banana bunchy top virus
  Control: Remove infected plants, control aphids with insecticides

MAJOR DISEASES:
- Fusarium Wilt (Panama Disease): Most serious, vascular wilting, no cure
  Control: Use resistant varieties (FHIA), destroy infected plants, quarantine
- Black Sigatoka: Black streaks on leaves, premature leaf death
  Control: Remove affected leaves, spray with fungicides (Propiconazole)
  Cultural: Good spacing, drainage, desuckering
- Yellow Sigatoka: Similar to Black but less severe
  Control: Remove affected leaves, fungicide sprays
- Banana Bunchy Top Virus (BBTV): Stunting, bunchy leaves
  Control: Remove infected plants immediately, control aphids
- Bacterial Wilt (Xanthomonas): Wilting, premature ripening, bacterial ooze
  Control: Use clean tools (sterilize with fire/disinfectant)
  Remove infected plants and suckers within 50m radius

INTEGRATED MANAGEMENT:
- Plant disease-free material
- Regular field scouting
- Remove diseased plants immediately
- Maintain good sanitation (remove dead leaves)
- Disinfect farm tools between plants
- Proper spacing for air circulation

==================================================
SECTION 8: SUPPORTING & PROPPING
==================================================

WHY SUPPORT IS NEEDED:
- Prevent toppling due to heavy bunches
- Wind damage prevention
- Shallow root system

PROPPING METHODS:
- Single pole prop: One strong pole against plant
- Forked prop: Y-shaped support under bunch
- Double prop: Two poles on opposite sides
- Cable/wire support: For large plantations

TIMING:
- Install props when bunch emerges
- Before fruit starts gaining weight
- Adjust as bunch develops

==================================================
SECTION 9: HARVESTING & POST-HARVEST
==================================================

TIME TO HARVEST:
- First harvest: 9-12 months for tissue culture
- First harvest: 12-15 months for suckers
- Subsequent harvests: Every 8-10 months

MATURITY INDICATORS:
- Fruits well-filled (fingers rounded, not angular)
- Flowers at finger tips dry and easily rubbed off
- Change in fruit color (green becomes lighter)
- 75-80% mature for export/long storage
- 90-100% mature for local market

HARVESTING PROCEDURE:
- Use sharp panga or knife
- Cut bunch with one clean stroke
- Cushion bunch fall with banana leaf or padding
- Leave 30cm of stalk attached to bunch
- Harvest in morning (cooler temperature)

DEHANDING:
- Separate hands from stalk
- Use clean, sharp knife
- Dip hands in clean water
- Grade by size and quality

POST-HARVEST HANDLING:
- Avoid bruising and cuts
- Wash fruits in clean water
- Treat with fungicide solution (optional)
- Dry in shade
- Pack in ventilated crates or cartons

RIPENING:
- Natural ripening: 5-7 days at room temperature
- Artificial ripening: Ethylene gas in ripening chambers
- Cooking bananas: Ripen in closed container with ripe fruits

STORAGE:
- Green bananas: 2-4 weeks at 13-15°C
- Ripe bananas: 5-7 days at room temperature
- Do not refrigerate (causes blackening)

SECTION 10: YIELD & ECONOMICS
==================================================

EXPECTED YIELD:
- First ratoon: 15-25 tons per acre per year
- Mature plantation: 25-40 tons per acre per year
- Tissue culture: Higher yields than suckers
- Good management: Up to 50 tons per acre

BUNCH WEIGHT:
- Plantain: 20-40kg per bunch
- Dessert banana: 15-30kg per bunch

MARKET PRICES (Seasonal variation):
- Cooking bananas: 30-60 KES per kg
- Dessert bananas: 40-80 KES per kg
- Export quality: 100-150 KES per kg

INCOME POTENTIAL:
- 30 tons × 50 KES = 1,500,000 KES per acre per year
- Continuous production throughout the year

PRODUCTION COSTS PER ACRE (ANNUAL):
- Planting material: 150,000-250,000 KES (one-time, tissue culture)
- Fertilizers: 60,000-80,000 KES
- Pesticides: 30,000-50,000 KES
- Labor: 100,000-150,000 KES
- Other costs: 30,000 KES
- Total (excluding planting): 220,000-310,000 KES

NET PROFIT (After establishment):
- 1,200,000-1,300,000 KES per acre per year
- Higher for export quality

SECTION 11: INTERCROPPING & AGROFORESTRY
==================================================

SUITABLE INTERCROPS:
- Coffee: Classic combination in Central Kenya
- Beans: Between rows (short-term)
- Yams/Cassava: Compatible root crops
- Passion fruit: Can use banana stems as support
- Vegetables: Lettuce, spinach, tomatoes (in shade)
- Napier grass: For livestock feed on borders

SPACING FOR INTERCROPPING:
- Plant bananas at wider spacing (3m × 3m)
- Allows space for intercrops
- Reduces competition

BENEFITS:
- Additional income
- Soil cover and erosion control
- Efficient land use
- Risk diversification

AVOID:
- Heavy feeders like maize (compete for nutrients)
- Tall crops that shade bananas
- Crops that harbor banana pests

==================================================
SECTION 12: VALUE ADDITION & MARKETS
==================================================

VALUE ADDITION:
- Banana flour: From green cooking bananas
- Banana chips: Fried or baked snacks
- Banana juice/wine: From ripe dessert bananas
- Dried bananas: Long shelf life
- Baby food: Pureed ripe bananas

FRESH MARKET:
- Local markets: Bulk buyers, retailers
- Supermarkets: Require consistent quality and supply
- Hotels and restaurants
- Direct sales to consumers
- Roadside sales

EXPORT MARKETS:
- European Union: Strict quality standards
- Middle East: Growing demand
- Requires GlobalGAP certification
- Consistent supply critical

PROCESSING:
- Contract with processing companies
- Banana flour factories
- Juice processors

==================================================
SECTION 13: SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
==================================================

CLIMATE CHANGE ADAPTATION:
- Use drought-tolerant varieties
- Mulching heavily (10-15cm layer)
- Rainwater harvesting
- Drip irrigation installation

ORGANIC PRODUCTION:
- Use only organic fertilizers (manure, compost)
- Biological pest control
- Neem-based pesticides
- Higher market prices (30-50% premium)

CERTIFICATION:
- GlobalGAP for export
- Kenya Organic Agriculture Network (KOAN)
- Fairtrade certification

BANANA CYCLE MANAGEMENT:
- Replace old plantations after 5-7 years
- Rejuvenate with tissue culture plantlets
- Soil improvement before replanting
- Prevents pest and disease buildup
